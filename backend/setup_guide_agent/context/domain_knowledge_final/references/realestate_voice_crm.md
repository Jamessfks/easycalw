A new class of AI is beginning to draw attention among tech geeks and innovators that could impact real estate: autonomous AI "agents" capable of managing complex workflows across multiple software platforms.

One of the most talked-about systems in developer circles is OpenClaw (formerly Moltbot, Clawdbot, Molty), an open-source platform that allows users to build AI assistants capable of executing tasks across apps, websites and databases.

While the tech remains largely experimental, Canadian real estate broker Daniel Foch says he has spent a month testing OpenClaw specifically for real estate workflows — and believes the industry could benefit significantly from it.

Foch is the Chief Real Estate Officer at Valery.ca and host of Canada's top-ranked real estate podcast. He is also the co-founder of The Habistat, the data science platform behind TRREB and Proptx.

"As far as I know, nobody in the real estate industry has spent more time trying to make OpenClaw work for Realtors than I have," Foch said. "I genuinely believe real estate stands to benefit more than any other industry with a technology like this."

## 'A field job with a desk backend'

Unlike typical AI chatbots that simply generate text or answer questions, OpenClaw is designed to perform tasks autonomously. The system can connect to software tools such as email platforms, calendars, databases and CRM systems.

"Real estate is a field job with a desk backend," Foch said. "Agents are in their cars, at showings, inspections, or listing appointments. They're not sitting in their CRM or their email all day."

## AI takes on the boring work

Foch says his customized OpenClaw system now handles a wide range of tasks:
- Update contacts and follow-up tasks inside a CRM
- Generate listing presentations and home evaluations
- Schedule showings and add them to his calendar
- Process paperwork and organize transaction documents
- Draft outreach campaigns to potential clients
- Generate content for marketing and media channels

In total, Foch estimates the AI assistant now handles roughly 70 percent to 80 percent of the administrative work that previously consumed 20 to 25 hours of his week.

## 'Screenless CRM management'

His system can interact with CRM tools commonly used by agents, including Follow Up Boss and kvCORE, using their APIs. He also created automations that allow the AI to manage lead follow-ups, trigger nurture campaigns and update client records.

In one example, he can send a text message to his AI assistant instructing it to move a contact to a nurture sequence and schedule a follow-up call. The AI then updates the CRM, triggers the appropriate automations and drafts the follow-up message. For agents who spend much of their time driving between showings, such "screenless CRM management" can be particularly valuable.

## Why most agents aren't using OpenClaw

Setting up the system requires technical knowledge, including configuring integrations, building workflows and defining detailed standard operating procedures.

Foch spent a month documenting the process and creating open-source "skills" that agents can use to replicate his setup. Even then, most agents conclude that building the system themselves would require too much time and technical expertise.

In response, Foch and several partners are developing a commercial product called Homie that packages OpenClaw-based automation into a ready-to-use system for real estate professionals.

"The industry doesn't necessarily need instructions," he said. "It needs packaging."

## An 'absolute nightmare' for cybersecurity

OpenClaw's powerful capabilities introduce significant cybersecurity risks, particularly when AI agents are granted access to files, system commands and third-party applications, according to security researchers at Cisco Systems.

"From a capability perspective, OpenClaw is groundbreaking. From a security perspective, it's an absolute nightmare," Cisco's Amy Chang and Vineeth Sai Narajala recently wrote.

Researchers have also reported instances where OpenClaw exposed plaintext API keys and credentials. Even OpenClaw's documentation acknowledges: "There is no perfectly secure setup."

Wired reported that Meta and several other tech companies have restricted the use of OpenClaw internally due to security concerns. However, Meta recently acquired Moltbook, the "social network" for AI agents built on OpenClaw.
